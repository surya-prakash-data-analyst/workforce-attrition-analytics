# Stakeholder Recommendations

## For HR Leadership

The single highest-leverage action is an **overtime policy review**. Employees working overtime churn at 30.6% — three times the 10.4% rate for those without. That gap is too large to ignore. The immediate step is auditing which teams and roles are generating the most overtime and whether it reflects poor planning, understaffing, or deadline pressure. Compensating overtime properly or redistributing workload would have measurable attrition impact within two quarters.

The second priority is **Sales Representative retention**. At 39.3% attrition, this role is hemorrhaging headcount in the department that directly generates revenue. Sales reps typically take 6–12 months to reach full productivity. The combination of departure cost, vacancy cost, and ramp time makes each Sales Rep departure significantly more expensive than the salary figure alone suggests. A dedicated career pathway, clearer promotion criteria, and recognition program specific to this role would address the root cause better than one-off retention bonuses.

**Recommended tracking metrics (monthly):**
- Overall attrition rate vs 16.1% baseline
- Department-level attrition rates — flag any above 20%
- Overtime headcount percentage by team
- Exit interview completion rate and sentiment

---

## For Department Managers (Sales)

Your department has the highest attrition rate in the company at 20.7%, led by Sales Representatives at 39.3%. Three factors in the data correlate most strongly with Sales attrition: low monthly income (many reps in the sub-$5K band), frequent business travel, and overtime.

Practical actions:
- Review compensation for Sales Representatives below $5,000/month — the data shows this band churns at 21.6%
- Implement a travel maximum (e.g., no more than 8 overnight trips per quarter) for field reps
- Create a visible career path from Sales Representative → Sales Executive → Manager with clear criteria

---

## For Department Managers (R&D)

R&D has the lowest attrition at 13.8%, but Laboratory Technicians at 23.8% are a notable exception. This role churns at nearly double the department average. Lab Technicians are often entry-level, low-salary, and do repetitive work with limited autonomy — a profile that produces high attrition across industries.

Practical actions:
- Introduce structured rotation programs for Lab Technicians to broaden skills
- Review compensation benchmarks for this role against market rates
- Create a clear technical career track (Technician → Senior Technician → Specialist) as an alternative to the management track

---

## For Compensation & Benefits

The salary band analysis is clear: employees earning below $5,000/month leave at 21.6% — compared to 3.8% for those above $15,000. While raising all salaries is not always feasible, targeted adjustments in the highest-risk roles would have the best ROI.

Additionally, the stock option data is worth reviewing. Employees with stock option level 0 leave more frequently than those with any options. Expanding stock option eligibility — even modest grants — to more employees in the sub-$5K band may improve retention at relatively low cost.

---

## For Talent Acquisition

The 18–25 age group churns at 35.8% — the highest of any cohort. This is expected in early career stages, but the rate suggests the company may not be offering what young employees need: mentorship, skills development, fast feedback cycles, and a clear picture of where their career is going.

A structured early career program (18-month rotational, dedicated mentors, quarterly check-ins) would reduce this attrition rate and build a stronger internal talent pipeline. Given that this cohort is 123 employees and 44 left in this dataset period, even retaining 10 additional people annually saves approximately $95,000 in replacement costs.
